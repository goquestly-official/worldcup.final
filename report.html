A statistical “what if these two met in the final” simulation of a neutral-venue FIFA World Cup final, fit to real historical international results.
This is a modeling exercise, not a prediction of a real scheduled match. It estimates how a hypothetical final might play out given each side’s recent form in the data — real football has injuries, tactics, and luck no Poisson model captures.

Methodology

Honest about what this is and isn’t.

Data source

Real historical international football results from the open-source martj42/international_results GitHub repository — the same dataset distributed on Kaggle as “International football results from 1872 to…”. 49,518 matches with scores spanning 1872-11-30 to 2026-07-15. Each record has date, home/away team, goals, tournament, and a neutral-venue flag.

Model

A weighted, ridge-regularised Poisson attack/defence model with a Dixon-Coles low-score correction. Each team gets an attack and a defence rating; expected goals are exp(μ + home·venue + attackteam + defenceopp). Fit only on the last 12 years (11,577 matches, 299 teams) with an exponential time-decay weight (3-year half-life) so recent form dominates. A World Cup final is neutral, so the estimated home-advantage term (≈1.27× goals) is removed for both sides. That yields neutral-venue expected goals of Argentina 1.03 and Spain 1.07.

Simulating one final

  • Regulation: a 90′ scoreline drawn from the fitted Dixon-Coles joint distribution (ρ = −0.05, mildly boosting low-scoring draws).
  • Extra time: if level, add 30′ of independent Poisson goals at ~1/3 the full-match rate.
  • Penalties: if still level, a shootout. Baseline is a coin flip, nudged slightly by each side’s historical shootout record (Argentina 15/23, Spain 7/14) — heavily damped toward 50/50 → P(Argentina) ≈ 0.553. A minor adjustment, not the story.

Why 500,000 simulations

The standard error of a proportion is √(p(1−p)/N). At N = 500k and p ≈ 0.5, that’s ≈ 0.07%, a 95% confidence interval of about ±0.14% on the headline win probabilities — tighter than the model’s own uncertainty, so more trials wouldn’t change any reported figure. A convergence check confirms stability: Argentina’s win share was 48.5% at N/10 (50k) and 48.8% at the independent full run — well inside the interval.

Because the two sides come out so evenly matched, the ±0.14% Monte Carlo noise means the winner tilts marginally between runs — the honest read is a genuine coin-flip final, not a Spain edge.
